Analogies Between HW4 and HW7

Let discuss the deep analogies between the Law of Large Numbers (HW4) and the Attacker Random Walk (HW7). Both simulations are, at their core, visualizations of the same fundamental concept: the Bernoulli Process.

Similarities and Differences

Both simulations involved running \(M\) independent trajectories, each consisting of \(N\) steps (trials). The primary difference is not in the process itself, but in what we measure and what we plot.

Concept HW4 HW7
Core Process Bernoulli Trial Bernoulli Trial
Single Step (Trial) Success (1) or Failure (0) Success (+1) or Failure (-1)
Probability (p) Variable (e.g., 0.5, 0.3, ...) Fixed at 0.5
Plot 1 (Paths) Frequency: \(f_n = S_n / n\) Score: \(Score_n = S_n - F_n\)
Plot 2 (Histogram) Distribution of final frequencies \(f_N\) Distribution of final scores \(Score_N\)

As the table shows, the Attacker Random Walk (HW7) is just a specific case of the general Bernoulli process (HW4). It is a "Simple Random Walk" because \(p=0.5\). The score \((+1, -1)\) is just a different way of mapping the outcome than the frequency \((1, 0)\).

Mathematical Properties and Relationships

The link between these processes and combinatorics is the Binomial Distribution, which is the foundation for all of them.

Binomial Coefficients & Pascal's Triangle

In HW7, we defined the final score after \(n\) steps as \(Score = 2S_n - n\), where \(S_n\) is the total number of successes.

The number of successes, \(S_n\), follows the Binomial Distribution. The probability of getting exactly \(k\) successes in \(n\) trials is:

\[ P(S_n = k) = C(n, k) \cdot p^k \cdot (1-p)^{n-k} \]

The term \(C(n, k)\) (or \(\binom{n}{k}\)) is the binomial coefficient, read "n choose k". It counts the number of different paths an attacker could take to end up with exactly \(k\) successes.

These coefficients are famously generated by Pascal's Triangle. Each number in the triangle is the sum of the two numbers directly above it. The \(n^{th}\) row of the triangle *is* the list of all possible values for \(C(n, k)\) (from \(k=0\) to \(k=n\)).

Therefore, the shape of our histogram in HW7 (and in HW4, if we set \(p=0.5\)) is not just *like* the \(n^{th}\) row of Pascal's Triangleâ€”it is a direct visualization of it, scaled by the probability \(p^k(1-p)^{n-k}\).

Binomial Expansion

The coefficients are named after the Binomial Theorem (or Expansion), which states:

\[ (x + y)^n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} C(n, k) \cdot x^{n-k} \cdot y^k \]

The relationship becomes clear if we set \(x = (1-p)\) (probability of failure) and \(y = p\) (probability of success):

\[ ((1-p) + p)^n = (1)^n = 1 \] \[ \sum_{k=0}^{n} C(n, k) \cdot (1-p)^{n-k} \cdot p^k = 1 \]

This shows that the Binomial Expansion is effectively the "generating function" for the Binomial Distribution. Each term in the sum, \(C(n, k) \cdot p^k \cdot (1-p)^{n-k}\), is precisely the probability of achieving \(k\) successes. The sum of all possible outcomes (from 0 to n successes) must be 1.

The Fibonacci Sequence

A surprising and elegant connection exists between Pascal's Triangle and the Fibonacci Sequence (0, 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, ...).

If you sum the "shallow diagonals" of Pascal's Triangle, the sums you get are the Fibonacci numbers.

While less direct, this connects our Random Walk simulation to another fundamental mathematical concept. The Fibonacci sequence appears in combinatorial problems that involve counting paths with specific constraints (e.g., "how many ways can you climb \(n\) stairs taking steps of 1 or 2?"). This highlights the deep shared roots of all path-counting problems, whether they are random walks (Binomial) or constrained paths (Fibonacci).
