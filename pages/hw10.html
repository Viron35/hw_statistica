Assignment: Poisson Arrival (Jump) Process Simulation

This assignment simulates a counting process \( \{N(t), t \ge 0\} \) over a time interval \( T \). Events occur independently and uniformly at a constant average rate \( \lambda \).

1. Approximation Method (Bernoulli to Poisson)

To simulate this continuous process using a computer (which is discrete), we divide the time interval \( T \) into \( n \) very small subintervals of length \( \Delta t = T/n \).

In each subinterval, we perform a Bernoulli trial:

  • Probability of success (event occurs): \( p = \lambda \cdot \Delta t = \lambda / n \).
  • Probability of failure (no event): \( 1 - p \).

The Logic: If \( n \) is very large (and thus \( \Delta t \) very small), the probability of getting more than one event in a single subinterval becomes negligible. The sum of these independent Bernoulli trials converges to a Poisson Process.

2. Analysis of the Resulting Process

The process generated is an approximation of the Homogeneous Poisson Process. It has the following theoretical properties:

  • Counting Process: \( N(t) \) represents the total number of arrivals up to time \( t \). It is a non-decreasing "step" function (as seen in the plot below).
  • Independent Increments: The number of events in disjoint time intervals are independent (the process is "memoryless").
  • Poisson Distribution: The total number of events in any interval of length \( t \) follows a Poisson distribution with parameter \( \lambda t \): \( P(N(t)=k) = e^{-\lambda t} \frac{(\lambda t)^k}{k!} \).
  • Inter-arrival Times: The time between consecutive jumps is exponentially distributed with parameter \( \lambda \).

Meaning of \( \lambda \) (Lambda): This is the rate intensity. It represents the expected average number of events per unit of time. For example, if \( \lambda = 50 \) and \( T=1 \), we expect roughly 50 jumps in the graph.
