Representation of the Law of Large Numbers

The Law of Large Numbers (LLN) is a fundamental theorem in probability. It guarantees that if I repeat an experiment a very large number of times, the observed average of the outcomes will get arbitrarily close to its theoretical expected value.

This relationship is defined by two concepts:

  • Theoretical Probability (p): A fixed, abstract number representing the "success" probability of a single trial.
  • Empirical Frequency (fn): An experimental value. It is the ratio of observed successes (Sn) after \(n\) trials: \(f_n = S_n / n\).

The LLN states that the error, or the difference \(|f_n - p|\), is guaranteed to approach 0 as the number of trials, \(n\), approaches infinity.

Interactive Simulation: Bernoulli Trials

To visualize these concepts, I created a simulation that runs **M** independent trajectories. Each trajectory consists of **N** Bernoulli trials with a success probability of **p**.

This simulation generates two distinct visualizations:

  1. Convergence Plot: Shows a sample of 10 trajectories (from the M total). This chart plots the empirical frequency \(f_n\) at each step \(n\) (from 1 to N). It illustrates the "path" of convergence and vividly displays the variance between different random trials.
  2. Histogram of Final Frequencies: This plots the distribution of the *final* frequencies (\(f_N\)) from all **M** trajectories. This visualization illustrates the **Central Limit Theorem**, showing that these final results cluster in a Normal (bell-shaped) distribution centered around the true probability \(p\).

Convergence Path (10 Sample Trajectories)

This chart displays 30 random paths. Notice how each trajectory (colored lines) is unique, but they all tend to "converge" or cluster around the theoretical probability 'p' (red line) as the number of trials (N) increases.

Histogram of Final Frequencies (M Trajectories)

This histogram shows the final frequency (at N trials) for all M trajectories. It forms a 'bell curve' (Normal Distribution) centered on the true probability 'p'. This shows that most experiments end up very close to the true value.
