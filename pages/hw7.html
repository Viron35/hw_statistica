Attacker Trajectories as Random Walks

This assignment simulates the activity of \(m\) attackers over \(n\) weeks. Each attacker attempts to breach a server every week, with a success probability \(p=0.5\).

I generated \(m\) random trajectories, where each trajectory represents the cumulative score of a single attacker.

1. The Simulation Model (Random Walk)

Each of the \(m\) attackers is tracked independently for \(n\) weeks. Each attacker starts with a score of 0.

Every week, each attacker:

  • Succeeds (with probability \(p=0.5\)): Their score increases by 1.
  • Fails (with probability \(1-p=0.5\)): Their score decreases by 1.

This process precisely describes a Simple 1D Random Walk (or "Drunkard's Walk"). Each attacker's score-path over \(n\) weeks is one "trajectory".

2. Convergence to the Binomial Distribution

I simulated \(m\) trajectories (one for each attacker) and plotted a histogram of their final scores after \(n\) weeks. This distribution of final scores is directly related to the Binomial Distribution.

For a single attacker, let \(S_n\) be the total number of "Successes" (+1) and \(F_n\) be the total number of "Failures" (-1) after \(n\) weeks.

  • Total weeks (trials): \(n = S_n + F_n\)
  • Final Score: \( \text{Score} = S_n - F_n \)

By substituting \(F_n = n - S_n\), we can define the score based only on successes:
\( \text{Score} = S_n - (n - S_n) \)
\( \text{Score} = 2S_n - n \)

Since each week is an independent trial with \(p=0.5\), the number of successes \(S_n\) for any attacker follows a Binomial distribution: \( S_n \sim \text{Binomial}(n, p=0.5) \).

This means our histogram of final scores (Plot 2) must converge to the shape of the Binomial distribution, just shifted (by \(-n\)) and scaled (by \(2\)). As \(m\) (the number of attackers/trajectories) diverges, the experimental histogram becomes a perfect match for the theoretical Binomial PMF (red line).
